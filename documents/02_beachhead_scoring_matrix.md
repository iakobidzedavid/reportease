## Scoring Criteria Definitions

### Criterion 1: TAM Size (Weight: 30%)
**Definition:** Total addressable market revenue potential (# of companies × avg users per company × annual ARPU).

**Scoring Scale (1-5):**
- **5 = Exceptional:** TAM >$50M; 50,000+ addressable users
- **4 = Strong:** TAM $25M-$50M; 15,000-50,000 users
- **3 = Moderate:** TAM $10M-$25M; 5,000-15,000 users
- **2 = Limited:** TAM $3M-$10M; 1,000-5,000 users
- **1 = Minimal:** TAM <$3M; <1,000 users

**Rationale:** Larger TAM provides more runway for growth and multiple customers for initial validation. However, we're weighting TAM at only 30% because addressing a smaller, accessible segment with product-market fit is preferable to a large, hard-to-reach one.

### Criterion 2: Accessibility (Weight: 25%)
**Definition:** Ease of reaching decision-makers, length of sales cycle, inbound interest level.

**Scoring Scale (1-5):**
- **5 = Extremely High:** <2 week sales cycle; decision-maker is founder/owner; high inbound interest via communities
- **4 = High:** 2-6 week sales cycle; direct access to CMO/director; moderate-to-strong inbound; word-of-mouth works
- **3 = Moderate:** 6-10 week sales cycle; access requires multi-stakeholder negotiation; limited inbound
- **2 = Low:** 10-16 week sales cycle; gatekeeper access required; minimal inbound; slow to adopt
- **1 = Very Low:** >16 weeks; requires executive recruitment; extremely low inbound

**Rationale:** A startup succeeds by rapidly iterating with customers. Inaccessible segments require expensive sales infrastructure before PMF is proven. We weight accessibility at 25% to ensure we can afford to sell.

### Criterion 3: Willingness to Pay (Weight: 25%)
**Definition:** Average revenue per user per year, based on company revenue, reporting budget, and time savings.

**Scoring Scale (1-5):**
- **5 = Very High:** WTP >$3,000/user/year; strong ROI perception; budget available
- **4 = High:** WTP $1,500-$3,000/user/year; clear ROI; moderate budget availability
- **3 = Moderate:** WTP $800-$1,500/user/year; decent ROI; budget constrained
- **2 = Low:** WTP $400-$800/user/year; budget-constrained; price-sensitive
- **1 = Very Low:** WTP <$400/user/year; minimal budget; extreme price sensitivity

**Rationale:** Willingness to pay directly impacts unit economics (LTV) and revenue potential. High WTP enables reinvestment in product and sales. We weight it at 25% to maintain balance with accessibility—high WTP with zero accessibility = failed startup.

### Criterion 4: Competitive Intensity (Weight: 15%)
**Definition:** Number of direct competitors, market saturation, ease of differentiation.

**Scoring Scale (1-5):**
- **5 = Low:** <3 direct competitors; high differentiation potential; new market category
- **4 = Moderate-Low:** 3-6 competitors; clear differentiation axes; room for 2-3 players
- **3 = Moderate:** 6-10 competitors; differentiation possible but requires execution; crowded
- **2 = High:** 10-15 competitors; limited differentiation; difficult to win share
- **1 = Very High:** >15 competitors; commodity market; price/feature parity with large players

**Rationale:** Early-stage companies need "blue ocean" opportunities where they can win without directly fighting entrenched players. We weight competitive intensity at only 15% because strong execution in a crowded market can win (see: Slack in chat, Figma in design). But all else equal, low competition is preferable.

### Criterion 5: Growth Rate (Weight: 5%)
**Definition:** Year-over-year market growth rate (headcount, revenue, units).

**Scoring Scale (1-5):**
- **5 = Exceptional:** >20% YoY growth; strong tailwinds
- **4 = Strong:** 10-20% YoY growth; moderate tailwinds
- **3 = Moderate:** 5-10% YoY growth; slight tailwinds
- **2 = Slow:** 2-5% YoY growth; flat or mature market
- **1 = Declining:** <2% YoY or negative growth; headwind

**Rationale:** Growing markets increase total TAM and create higher volumes to close (easier to sell when customer base is expanding). However, we weight growth at only 5% because the most important metric is beachhead fit—entering a declining segment with PMF beats a growing segment without fit. Note: we're avoiding growth-at-all-costs thinking here.

## Beachhead Selection Justification

### Why Digital Marketing Agencies (10-50 people) is the Optimal Beachhead

**Primary Rationale:**
Digital Marketing Agencies score **4.2/5.0** on the weighted beachhead matrix — the highest of all 12 segments. This score is driven by:

1. **High Accessibility (4.5/5.0):** Direct access to decision-makers (agency owners, CMOs) via warm outreach, industry networks, and word-of-mouth. Sales cycle is 2-6 weeks, enabling rapid validation and iteration. This is critical for a pre-Series A startup with limited brand and cash.

2. **Strong Willingness to Pay (4.0/5.0):** Agencies have clear ROI perception. Saving 6 hrs/week per team member × $50/hr loaded cost = $3,600/year in value created. At $1,200-$1,800/year pricing, the value-to-price ratio is 2-3X, making purchasing decisions straightforward.

3. **Solid TAM Size (4.0/5.0):** TAM of $25.5M (8,500 companies × 2.5 users × $1,200 ARPU) is large enough to build a $50M+ ARR company (assuming 60%+ penetration in 5 years), but not so large that we're lost competing against enterprise software vendors with 500+ person sales teams.

4. **Manageable Competitive Intensity (3.5/5.0):** 3-4 direct competitors (Supermetrics, Data Box, Agency Analytics) exist, but differentiation is possible through:
   - **Ease of use:** Competing products are feature-heavy and complex; we can win on simplicity + speed of implementation
   - **White-label + Multi-client dashboards:** Agencies want to white-label reports for clients; most competitors don't offer this well
   - **Scheduling + Automation:** Set-and-forget reporting with alerts and automated client distribution is a differentiator
   - **Integration breadth:** Integrating with Google Analytics, HubSpot, Facebook Ads, LinkedIn Ads, etc. creates a moat

5. **Positive Growth Rate (4.0/5.0):** 9% YoY headcount growth in digital agencies + 9% CAGR market growth provides tailwinds. Expanding agencies need more reporting staff or tools to automate the work.

### Comparison Against Top 2 Alternatives

#### Alternative #1: Performance Marketing Agencies (4.0/5.0)
**Strengths:**
- Highest accessibility (5.0/5.0); fastest sales cycle (1-3 weeks)
- Highest willingness to pay among same segment (agencies)
- Very acute pain: daily/weekly reporting for 8-25 clients

**Weaknesses:**
- Smaller TAM ($29.4M vs. $25.5M) — actually comparable
- Fewer companies (4,200 vs. 8,500) but more users per company (3.5 vs. 2.5)
- More specialized positioning limits follow-on market expansion

**Verdict:** Performance Marketing Agencies score nearly as high (4.0 vs. 4.2). This is actually a viable **co-beachhead** positioning, where we target both "full-service digital agencies" and "performance marketing specialists." For Phase 1, we recommend starting with **full-service agencies** (broader TAM) but then rapidly adding performance marketing as a sub-segment in the marketing messaging.

#### Alternative #2: SaaS/Tech Companies (3.9/5.0)
**Strengths:**
- Highest growth rate (25-35% YoY); strongest tailwinds
- Second-highest willingness to pay ($2,000-$3,500)
- High accessibility to VP Marketing

**Weaknesses:**
- Smaller TAM ($42M vs. $25.5M)
- Highest competitive intensity; VC-backed competitors (Amplitude, Segment) have unlimited capital
- Require more technical sophistication to differentiate (data modeling, API integrations)
- Sales cycle is 3-6 weeks but requires more proof-of-concept work

**Verdict:** SaaS/Tech is a strong **secondary beachhead** (Phase 2-3) but not ideal for Phase 1. The beachhead selection framework prioritizes accessibility over growth rate—and agencies are more accessible to a bootstrapped/pre-seed startup than VCs-backed SaaS founders with procurement teams.

### Why NOT the Large TAM Segments?

**In-House Marketing (50-200 people): 3.2/5.0**
- Despite TAM of $315M (largest), accessibility is poor (2.5/5.0)
- Sales cycle is 6-12 weeks; requires multi-stakeholder alignment
- Willingness to pay is only $800-$1,200 (lowest of primary segments)
- Must compete directly against Tableau, Power BI, Looker (massive incumbents)
- **Verdict:** Excellent Phase 3-4 market (follow-on expansion) AFTER PMF with agencies

**Retail/Omnichannel (50+ locations): 3.3/5.0**
- TAM is large ($61M) and willingness to pay is high ($2,000-$4,000)
- BUT sales cycle is 8-12 weeks and IT gatekeeping is high friction
- Must compete against enterprise BI vendors with sales teams + implementations
- **Verdict:** Phase 3-4 market after establishing agencies as reference customers

### Why AVOID Regulated Segments?

**Healthcare/Pharma (2.2/5.0) and Insurance/Financial Services (2.4/5.0)**
- Despite very high willingness to pay ($5,000-$15,000), accessibility is extremely poor
- Sales cycles are 12-24 weeks; regulatory gatekeeping (Chief Compliance Officer, Chief Information Security Officer) creates friction
- Require HIPAA, PCI-DSS, SOC 2 certifications BEFORE first sales conversation
- Incumbent vendors (Veeva, Salesforce regulated editions) have switching costs and compliance certifications
- **Verdict:** Explicitly avoid as beachhead. Revisit ONLY after:
  1. Establishing strong PMF with agencies
  2. Raising Series A capital (12-18 month sales cycle requires runway)
  3. Building compliance certifications
